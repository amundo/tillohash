A Little Introduction to Interlinear Glossing

Or: "Not right now, I'm @#%ing glossing!!"

This is an introduction to linguistic glossing for the uninitiated.

Here's a sentence in Cupeño.

Cupeño is a language from Southern California. There's a great grammar available online, from whence this example was chosen at random, page 229.
  1. Mu=ku'ut pe'=e aya pe-yawichi-qal a-yka, ne-t pe-ki̱yka.
  2. Mu=ku'ut pe'=e aya pe-yawichi-qal a-yka, ne-t pe-ki̱yka.

"Great," I hear you saying, "Why are there equals signs all over the place, and why is there a little line under the i in the last word, and what the heck does it mean?"

Fair questions all. And there are a lot more questions that might be asked: Who said it? When? Why? What does it actually sound like?

Let's start by just by considering the translation:

  1. Mu=ku'ut pe'=e aya pe-yawichi-qal a-yka, ne-t pe-ki̱yka.
  2. And it is said he would take it there, to the chief's house.

Hmm, okay. At least now we have some idea of what's being said.

But how does this Cupeño sentence work? Even with a translation, we're still just staring at a string of letters, without any understanding of what they mean. Presumably, somewhere in there is either a word, or a part of a word, that means "chief," and another that means something like "house," and others to do with "taking" and another "saying." But what's what?

If we're going to learn anything about how this language is put together by comparing these two sentences, someone who knows is going to have to label the bits and pieces of the Cupeño words. And that's where "glossing" comes in.

Old School Glossing

It's called "interlinear glossing," because, well, because it interlinear—a line in the language, a line of glossing. This particular glossed text is in an old style which is sometimes referred to as "word-by-word" glossing. It has the interesting characteristic that you can sort of read the text right away, and still get at least a vague feeling for how the language works. If you piece the glosses together into "prose" you end up with a sort of quasi-English:

People house they make it. Post they set it down, and here again they set it down, yonder again they set it down, in four places they set it down, in four places they set them down. Then also they place (beams) across on top thereof in four places, and on top thereof just once they place (beam) across. Then and just house its wall they make it; then and on top thereof they put them house boards. Sugar-pine those boards they make them.

This syntax is twisty from an English perspective, to be sure. (Looks a little like what comes out of a machine translation system, come to think of it.) But even from this literal and rather weird translation, you can get some feel for "where things go" in Talkema. For instance, there is clearly a similarity in structure between People house they make it and Sugar-pine those boards they make them. We can start to build up a little intuition about how clauses hang together.

Such labels have the advantage of being precise, but the disadvantage of not being self-explanatory. Does OBL stand for "Oblique"? Does GEN stand for "Genitive"? Probably, on both counts—but you're going to have to double check before you can read that gloss with any understanding.
