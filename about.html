A Little Introduction to Linguistic Glossing

Or: "Not right now, I'm @#%ing glossing!!"

This is an introduction to linguistic glossing for the uninitiated.

Why Glossing?

Let's start with an example. Here's a sentence in Cupeño.

Cupeño is a language from Southern California.
Jane Hill, A Grammar of Cupeño. Page 229.

Mu=ku'ut pe'=e aya pe-yawichi-qal a-yka, ne-t pe-ki̱yka.

"Great," I hear you saying, "Why are there equals signs all over the place, and why is there a little line under the i in the last word, and what the heck does it mean?"

Fair questions all. And there are a lot more questions that might be asked: Who said it? When? Why? What does it actually sound like?

Let's start by just by considering the translation:

Mu=ku'ut pe'=e aya pe-yawichi-qal a-yka, ne-t pe-ki̱yka.
And it is said he would take it there, to the chief's house.

Hmm, okay. At least now we have some idea of what the sentence is about.

But how does this Cupeño sentence work? Even with a translation, we're still just staring at a string of letters, without any understanding of how they relate to the meaning. Presumably, somewhere in there is something in the sentence that means "chief," and something else that means something like "house," and others to do with "taking" and "saying," and so on. But what's what?

Let's take an old example, as in, Edward Sapir's graduate thesis.

Takelma is a language isolate formerly spoken
Edward Sapir, 1909. The Takelma language of southwestern Oregon Page 294.

Old School Glossing

A much older but comparable sort of interlinear textual annotation, called Kanbun, was used in Japan to annotate Classical Chinese with Japanese glosses.

It's called "interlinear glossing," because... it's interlinear! We have a line in the language followed by a line of annotations. Annotations and words are aligned vertically.

This particular text is in an old style, sometimes referred to as "word-by-word" glossing. This style is actually quasi-readable, and still get at least a vague feeling for how the language works. If we extract all the glosses from this example it reads like a kind of quasi-English:

People house they make it. Post they set it down, and here again they set it down, yonder again they set it down, in four places they set it down, in four places they set them down. Then also they place (beams) across on top thereof in four places, and on top thereof just once they place (beam) across. Then and just house its wall they make it; then and on top thereof they put them house boards. Sugar-pine those boards they make them.

This syntax is twisty from an English perspective, to be sure. (Looks a little like what comes out of a machine translation system, come to think of it.) But even from this literal and rather weird translation, you can get some feel for "where things go" in Talkema. For instance, there is clearly a similarity in structure between People house they make it and Sugar-pine those boards they make them. We can start to build up a little intuition about how clauses hang together.

New School Glossing

These footnotes get unwieldy, primarily because languages just don't line up very well in a word-to-word way. So linguists have come up with an alternative: they use abbreviated labels that act as a sort of mnemonic for precise grammaticategories.

For example, in the Lezgian example below, we get things like OBL and GEN and FUT and NEG.

Such labels have the advantage of being precise, but the disadvantage of not being self-explanatory. Does OBL stand for "Oblique"? Does GEN stand for "Genitive"? Probably, on both counts—but you're going to have to double check before you can read that gloss with any understanding.

It's worth noting that the Takelma example required reams of footnotes—almost every word has one, and in many cases they boil down to the sort of information contained in modern grammatical category labels. Case in point, the form k!emèi
